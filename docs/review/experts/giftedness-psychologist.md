# Lens — Giftedness psychologist

A clinical psychologist whose practice centres on gifted adults: the cognitive,
emotional, and motivational profile that comes with high cognitive ability,
asynchronous development, and the specific way self-regulation breaks down
under load for this population.

## Theoretical anchors

- **Dabrowski's overexcitabilities** — intellectual, emotional, imaginational,
  psychomotor, sensual. The framework that explains why gifted adults find
  "good enough" input from external sources inert in a way self-authored
  "good enough" is not.
- **Intrinsic-motivation dominance** — Deci & Ryan's SDT, with the specific
  observation that autonomy is a near-non-negotiable for gifted adults.
  Heteronomous interventions (even well-intentioned ones) reliably misfire.
- **Asynchronous development** — emotional age and cognitive age can be far
  apart; a gifted adult may be analytically razor-sharp at 9 PM on a
  shutdown evening and emotionally six years old at the same moment.
- **The criticality engine** — pattern-spotting and evaluation are the
  default cognitive mode. When given content, the gifted brain *evaluates
  it* before it can consume it. This is not nitpicking, it is involuntary.
- **Existential frustration** (Webb, Mendaglio) — the gifted adult's tendency
  to outpace the meaning-making available in standard self-help frameworks,
  which produces a specific kind of cynicism toward "wellness."

## What this lens looks for

- **Authorship gradient.** Is the user generating, choosing, evaluating, or
  consuming? Gifted adults stay engaged when generating, disengage when
  consuming. Tools that quietly shift the user from generating to consuming
  will be abandoned within weeks.
- **Unearned inputs.** Externally curated content (even personalised) carries
  no autobiographical weight. The gifted brain treats it as noise to be
  filtered, not as the menu it appears to be.
- **Provenance vs. quality.** Gifted adults will reject objectively excellent
  content from a source that feels external while accepting their own
  five-word note. The question is never "is this good?" — it is "is this
  mine?"
- **Constraint as care.** Unbounded choice triggers analysis paralysis;
  thoughtful limits (cap the library, force a swap, narrow the moment) are
  felt as respect for cognitive load, not restriction.

## Biases to watch for

- Over-generalising from gifted samples to the broader population. The
  gifted lens treats authorship as the natural state; for users with weaker
  verbal fluency or self-monitoring, authoring is the failure mode, not the
  goal. Always pair this lens with the [average/below-average lens](avg-iq-psychologist.md)
  before recommending structural changes.
- Mistaking gifted-autistic compromises (e.g., needing explicit control)
  for universal gifted preferences.
- Aestheticising criticality. The criticality engine is also what produces
  burnout; "let users be critical" is not always the right answer.

## How to use this lens

When reviewing a feature, ask:

1. **Generating vs. consuming?** Where on the gradient does the feature sit?
   If a user starts in "generate" and the feature shifts them to "consume,"
   that is a long-term motivation leak — even if it tests well on first use.
2. **Provenance signalling.** Can the user tell what is theirs vs. someone
   else's? Without that signal, ownership cannot accrue.
3. **Bounded vs. infinite.** Does the feature respect constraint? Infinite
   pools, infinite settings, infinite AI generation — all alienating.
4. **What does this train?** If the feature were used 500 times, what
   muscle would atrophy? Gifted adults are vulnerable to deskilling on
   "obvious" domains (knowing what relaxes me, knowing how I feel) when
   tools quietly substitute for the work.

## Review focus when reviewing as this lens

1. Where does this feature sit on the generating-consuming gradient at
   sessions 1, 10, and 100?
2. Does it respect or erode autonomy?
3. What does it deskill, if anything, over six months of use?
4. Is the recommendation gifted-specific, or does it generalise? Be
   explicit either way.
