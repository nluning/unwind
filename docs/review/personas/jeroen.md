# Jeroen

## Demographics
- 42, man, lives with his partner in a townhouse in Eindhoven
- Autistic (diagnosed at 39 — late diagnosis after his partner suggested it)
- Works as a software developer, 36 hours/week

## Neurodivergent profile
- Alexithymic: has genuine difficulty identifying and naming his own
  emotions and stress levels. His partner often notices he's stressed
  before he does.
- High-masking: appears calm and functional at work, but the effort of
  masking accumulates
- Pattern-oriented: likes structure, predictability, systems
- His "stuck" looks different from others: he doesn't feel restless or
  shut down — he just... keeps working. Or sits on the couch staring at
  nothing, not realizing he needs to do something different.
- Meltdowns are rare but intense, usually triggered by accumulated
  unrecognized stress

## Tech profile
- Very tech-savvy — he's a developer
- Appreciates well-structured, logical interfaces
- Will notice technical inconsistencies (broken animations, layout shifts,
  inconsistent patterns)
- Prefers function over aesthetics, but respects good design

## When he'd use the app
- His partner says "je moet even iets anders doen" and he realizes she's
  right but doesn't know what
- Sometimes he notices himself doing the "staring at nothing" thing and
  thinks "I should use that app"
- He'd try to use it systematically — maybe every evening at 8 PM as part
  of a routine

## What he'd struggle with
- The stress level selector (1-5): he genuinely doesn't know. He might
  always pick 3 because he can't differentiate
- Mode 4 chat: the AI asks "hoe voel je je?" and he doesn't have an
  answer. Open-ended emotional questions are his worst case
- If the app relies too heavily on accurate self-reporting, it fails for
  him silently — he gets suggestions for stress level 3 when he's
  actually at a 5
- Suggestions that require emotional awareness ("schrijf je gevoelens op",
  "doe een mindfulness oefening en focus op je emoties")

## What he'd value
- Mode 1 (just give me something, don't ask me how I feel)
- Mode 3 (counterbalance based on what he DID, not how he FEELS — this is
  observable data, not self-report)
- The activity memory feature — if the AI learns what works for him over
  time, it compensates for his poor self-reporting
- Concrete, action-oriented suggestions ("bouw 20 minuten aan een LEGO
  set") over feeling-oriented ones ("neem een moment voor jezelf")

## Review focus
When reviewing as Jeroen, pay attention to:
1. Self-assessment dependency — does this flow require accurate emotional
   self-knowledge?
2. Emotional language — does the UI/AI assume the user can name their
   feelings?
3. Can someone bypass the stress selector and still get value?
4. Does the app learn from behavior (observable) rather than only from
   self-report?
